Refining Details

A natural inclination of designers is to make things look “pretty”. This is not a bad urge. The problem is when we spend time refining a solution which is fundamentally wrong. When you browse Dribbble, you notice lots of lovely things, but they are often missing a purpose or failing to meet a need.

However, when you finally build something worthwhile, you’ll want to turn it from an ugly duckling into a swan. And that’s the right order of things.

In late 2014, I joined Sonatype as a UX developer. They were in the middle of the next major version of Nexus, a repository management tool for developers. Nexus is a tool used by tens of thousands of people. It is a fantastic tool that is fundamental to how development is done. When I started, it looked something like this:

This is a perfect example of valuable tool, ugly duckling. As a designer and front-end developer, this was a perfect opportunity to give Nexus a visual makeover and make it easier to use at the same time.

Styling improvements

By the time Nexus 3 was released, it looked like this:

To get it to this point, I made a number of refinements. First, I shifted the colors to draw the eye to the most relevant part of the interface. White is a bright color, and I reserved it for the header and content area of the selected feature. Other areas of the interface received darker shades of grey to deemphasize them.

Next, I removed the border underneath the feature icon and title to reflect the way the tool scrolls. In a typical grid view, the icon, title, action buttons, and filter input are fixed to the top. The grid scrolls. To make this relationship clearer, I gave the aforementioned elements a shared white background.

The header received the next batch of changes. The active mode is indicated by a blue background. Because the active mode is such an important visual clue for orientation, I made it full bleed in the header. I also tied it to the rest of the page by adding a caret.

In addition, I revised the header icons from grey to white. This boosted contrast and gave the modes more pop. Finally, I aligned the version number with the baseline of the header title to make the overall effect more cohesive.

With respect to the content area itself, you might notice that M2 had significantly more buttons above the grid. All of these buttons, save one, require that a grid row be selected first. To simplify the UI and make this relationship clearer, I removed the buttons from this view and added them to the detail panel that appears when a row is selected.

Typography played another key role in the improvements. First, we updated the font itself to Proxima Nova, used in our other products. Then, we boosted the size of the feature title to help orient people.

The last thing you might notice is the right-hand carets, one per row in the grid. These were added as an affordance to hint at the result of clicking a row in the grid. I’ll describe this drilldown interaction more thoroughly in the next section.

Replace modals with drilldown

Modals are often a case of lazy design. A common scenario is when you want to enforce a linear workflow. In M2, we used them extensively. One of my goals was to replace modals with a better-designed alternative.

The alternative I chose is something I call “drilldown”. Instead of a modal, when you make a selection, the panel slides to the left and your selection slides in from the right. It includes a breadcrumb, so you can easily navigate back, and supports multiple levels of drilldown. This is handy when designing a wizard that consists of multiple steps.
